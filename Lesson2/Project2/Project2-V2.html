Conclusion:¶

Initial Questions:

  1. How have the number of homeruns changed over the course of time?
  2. Are there any external factors that could have influenced changes in number of homeruns hit by the homerun leader?
  3. Are the records of McGwire and Bonds that unusual? Possibly when compared to their historical homerun numbers or the other homerun leaders?

The number of homeruns per player has generally increased over time as has the number of homeruns of the homerun leader. However, there are some external factors that may have affected this change. The cork core ball was introduced which lead to the end of the "dead-ball" era, the spit-ball and other wild pitches were banned, and the number of games each team played increased.

As the number of games and at bats changed for different players, the correlation between AB and HR was examined and found to be moderately strong and positive. This may imply (as intuition would suggest) that if a player has a higher number of at bats then they would have a greater number of homeruns. So instead of investigating simply HR we investigate HR/AB.

When comparing to other players HR/AB and to their own historical HR/AB values, the Bonds and McGwire data points do not seem that unusual. Their career HR/AB average is among the highest but is not the highest. The data points do appear more unusual when we use instead just HR values. The record setting year for Bonds in 2001 was an anomoly but the high HR years for McGwire are not that unusual.
